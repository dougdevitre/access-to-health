# Population Deep Dive: Black / African American (BLK)

> This is not a health education sheet. This is operational guidance for professionals serving Black communities.

---

## Health Profile

### Leading Health Disparities
- **Cardiovascular disease**: 30% higher mortality than white Americans. Hypertension prevalence ~56% (vs. ~48% white). Earlier onset, more severe, less controlled
- **Maternal mortality**: 3-4x higher than white women. Driven by structural racism, provider bias, delayed diagnosis of complications, weathering (cumulative stress toll)
- **Infant mortality**: 2.3x higher than white infants. Black preterm birth rate ~14% vs. ~9% white
- **Diabetes**: 1.7x higher prevalence. Higher complication rates (amputation, kidney failure, blindness)
- **Cancer**: Higher incidence and mortality for most cancers. Later-stage diagnosis due to screening access gaps. Breast cancer mortality 40% higher despite similar incidence
- **HIV/AIDS**: 8x higher HIV incidence than white Americans. 43% of new diagnoses despite 13% of population
- **Mental health**: Similar prevalence but 50% less likely to receive treatment. Misdiagnosis rates higher (psychosis diagnosed when depression/anxiety is presenting)
- **Gun violence**: Leading cause of death for Black males ages 15-34. Concentrated in neighborhoods with decades of disinvestment
- **COVID-19**: 1.7x higher death rate. Driven by occupational exposure, housing density, comorbidities from structural racism, and health care access
- **Life expectancy gap**: Up to 20 years shorter in predominantly Black census tracts vs. white tracts in the same metro area

### Root Causes — Structural, Not Individual
These disparities are not caused by genetics or behavior. They are caused by:
1. **Residential segregation**: Redlining created and maintains racially concentrated poverty. Segregated neighborhoods have fewer grocery stores, more pollution, less green space, worse schools, and fewer providers
2. **Employment discrimination**: Lower wages, fewer benefits, more hazardous occupations, less workplace flexibility
3. **Educational inequity**: Underfunded schools, less access to health education, lower college completion rates affecting health literacy and employment
4. **Criminal justice system**: Mass incarceration disrupts families, eliminates employment and housing options, creates chronic stress. Incarceration is a social determinant of health
5. **Health care system bias**: Implicit bias in provider decision-making (pain management, referral patterns, diagnostic assumptions). Historical and ongoing medical exploitation
6. **Weathering**: Chronic exposure to racism causes physiological stress response, accelerating biological aging. A 30-year-old Black woman may have the physiological stress load of a 40-year-old white woman
7. **Environmental racism**: Proximity to highways, industrial facilities, waste sites. Higher lead exposure. Worse air quality
8. **Political disenfranchisement**: Voter suppression reduces political power to change health-harming policies

## Historical Context — Why Trust Is Low

Understanding these events is essential for any professional serving Black communities:

- **Tuskegee Syphilis Study (1932-1972)**: US Public Health Service deliberately withheld treatment from 399 Black men with syphilis for 40 years to study disease progression. Men were told they were receiving treatment. This is not ancient history — participants' children and grandchildren are alive today
- **Henrietta Lacks (1951)**: Cervical cancer cells taken without consent. Used for decades of medical research generating billions in profit. Family received nothing
- **J. Marion Sims**: "Father of gynecology" performed experimental surgeries on enslaved Black women without anesthesia
- **Forced sterilization**: Tens of thousands of Black women sterilized without consent through 1970s, including under Medicaid-funded programs
- **Crack vs. cocaine sentencing disparity**: Identical substances punished at 100:1 ratio, devastating Black communities
- **Flint, MI water crisis**: Predominantly Black city had lead-contaminated water for years while government minimized the risk
- **COVID-19 vaccine rollout**: Black communities received vaccines later despite higher mortality. Structural access barriers (pharmacy deserts, appointment-only systems, website-only registration) replicated existing inequities

**Bottom line**: Distrust of health systems is rational, earned, and ongoing. Trust must be rebuilt through sustained action, not just messaging.

## Cultural Considerations

### Communication
- **Direct address**: Many Black patients prefer direct, honest communication over euphemism. "The test showed cancer" not "There are some concerning findings"
- **Respect titles**: Address adults as Mr., Mrs., Ms. until invited to use first names. Especially elders
- **Code-switching awareness**: Black patients may communicate differently with providers than within community. Don't assume clinical demeanor reflects how they feel
- **Call and response**: In community settings (churches, town halls), participatory communication is normal and welcome
- **Storytelling**: Narrative is a primary way health information is shared and processed in many Black communities

### Faith
- The Black church is the most trusted institution in many Black communities. Health ministries, parish nursing, and pastor endorsement are powerful engagement tools
- Don't assume all Black people are religious — but recognize the church's central role
- Some health decisions are framed through spiritual lens ("God's will," prayer as healing). Respect this AND provide medical information. They are not mutually exclusive

### Family and Community
- Extended family networks often drive health decisions. Include family in care planning
- Community accountability: "How is this program helping my community?" matters more than individual benefit framing
- Barbershops and beauty salons are trusted third spaces for health conversations (evidence-based model: barber-based hypertension programs reduce BP by 20+ mmHg)

### What NOT to Do
- Do NOT assume poverty, single parenthood, or lack of education
- Do NOT treat race as a risk factor ("being Black increases your risk of..."). Instead: "Structural racism creates conditions that increase disease burden in Black communities"
- Do NOT use "non-compliant." Address barriers to care — transportation, cost, side effects, trust, competing priorities
- Do NOT use Black suffering as a data point without action. If you present disparity data, present your plan to address it
- Do NOT assume cultural homogeneity — Black communities include Caribbean, African, Southern, Northern, rural, urban, LGBTQ+, wealthy, poor, and everything in between

## Engagement Strategies That Work

### Trusted Messengers
- Black church leaders (pastors, deacons, health ministry leaders)
- Black barbers and hair stylists
- Black physicians and nurses (hire, don't just recruit for advisory boards)
- Community organizers and activists (not just nonprofit leaders)
- Black Greek organizations (Divine Nine)
- Black media (radio, newspapers, social media influencers)
- Elders and grandmothers

### Settings
- Churches and faith communities
- Barbershops and beauty salons (evidence-based model for hypertension, HIV testing)
- Community centers and recreation facilities
- Historically Black colleges and universities (HBCUs)
- Black-owned businesses
- Juneteenth, MLK Day, and cultural celebrations
- Block parties and neighborhood events
- Schools with predominantly Black student bodies

### Program Design Principles
1. **Community ownership**: Programs designed WITH, not FOR, Black communities
2. **Black leadership**: Black people in decision-making roles, not just advisory
3. **Asset-based**: Celebrate strengths — resilience, family networks, faith, cultural expression
4. **Address structural causes**: Don't just treat symptoms of racism — name it and work to change it
5. **Consistency**: Show up year after year, not just during grant cycles
6. **Accountability**: Report back to the community on what you promised and what you delivered

## Screening Protocol Adaptations

### Hypertension
- Screen at EVERY encounter (not just annual visits). Earlier onset in Black populations
- Use validated instruments calibrated for Black patients (some pulse oximeters and eGFR calculations have racial bias — use race-free eGFR)
- Home blood pressure monitoring: Provide cuffs and education
- Community screening: Barbershop model, church screening, mobile units in underserved zip codes

### Maternal Health
- Screen for depression at every prenatal AND postpartum visit (not just once)
- Screen for intimate partner violence in a private setting
- Ask about experiences of discrimination in health care: "Have you ever felt that a doctor or nurse treated you unfairly because of your race?"
- Assess birth plan preferences — many Black women want to avoid unnecessary interventions given history of medical exploitation

### Cancer
- Colorectal: Screen at 45 (new ACS recommendation). Black adults have higher incidence and mortality
- Breast: Mammography starting at 40. Address barriers: Transportation, childcare, fear of results, cost, distrust
- Prostate: Shared decision-making starting at 40-45 for Black men (higher risk)
- Cervical: Standard screening with emphasis on access — many Black women face barriers to routine gynecological care

### Mental Health
- Use culturally validated screening tools when available
- Normalize mental health in the context of systemic stress: "Given everything your community is dealing with, it makes sense that you might feel overwhelmed"
- Address misdiagnosis risk: Black patients are more likely to be diagnosed with schizophrenia when presenting with PTSD or depression. Advocate for accurate diagnosis
- Offer Black therapists when available. Use cultural matching

## Missouri / St. Louis Context

- North St. Louis City and North St. Louis County have some of the widest health disparity gaps in the nation
- The Delmar Divide: Life expectancy differs by 18 years across a single street separating predominantly Black (north) and predominantly white (south) neighborhoods
- Ferguson effect: 2014 police shooting of Michael Brown increased national attention to structural racism. Health implications: Chronic stress, community trauma, displacement
- Key organizations: Urban League of Metropolitan St. Louis, Fathers and Families Support Center, Better Family Life, Beloved Community, Beyond Housing, Places for People, Grace Hill, Mathews-Dickey Boys' and Girls' Club, Annie Malone Children and Family Service Center
