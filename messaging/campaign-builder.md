# Campaign Builder — 6 Health Campaigns + Planning Framework

> Ready-to-deploy campaign blueprints for common public health topics
> Each includes: audience, channels, key messages, timeline, metrics, and equity lens

---

## Campaign Planning Framework

### 5-Step Process
1. **Define**: What behavior or outcome are you trying to change? For whom?
2. **Research**: What does the audience know, believe, feel, and do? What are the barriers?
3. **Create**: Messages, materials, and channels matched to the audience
4. **Implement**: Phased rollout with built-in feedback loops
5. **Evaluate**: Did it work? What do we change?

## Campaign 1: Childhood Immunization

**Goal**: Increase up-to-date immunization rates for kindergarten entry

**Audiences**:
- Primary: Parents of children ages 0-5
- Secondary: Pediatricians, family medicine providers, childcare staff

**Key Messages**:
- "Vaccines protect your child and every child in the classroom"
- "Free vaccines are available at [locations] — no insurance needed"
- "On-time vaccines mean on-time school entry"

**Equity Lens**:
- Materials in top 3 community languages
- Address historical trust issues in Black communities (see `populations/deep-dives/black-african-american.md`)
- Partner with trusted messengers (pediatricians, faith leaders, WIC nutritionists)
- Mobile clinics in underserved zip codes
- Evening and weekend hours

**Channels**: Pediatric offices (posters, handouts), WIC appointments, childcare centers, faith communities, social media (Facebook for parents, Instagram for younger parents), school enrollment packets, community events

**Timeline**: January-August (before school year)

**Metrics**: Immunization coverage rate, # vaccines administered, # catch-up clinics held, kindergarten exemption rate

## Campaign 2: Naloxone / Overdose Prevention

**Goal**: Increase community naloxone access and reduce overdose deaths

**Audiences**:
- Primary: People who use drugs, their families and friends
- Secondary: First responders, faith communities, service providers

**Key Messages**:
- "Naloxone saves lives. It's free, safe, and available without a prescription in Missouri"
- "You can't get in trouble for calling 911 for an overdose" (Good Samaritan law)
- "Carry it like you'd carry a first aid kit — you might save a life"

**Equity Lens**:
- Reduce stigma: Person-first language ("person who uses drugs" not "addict")
- Distribution in communities most affected (not just suburban pharmacies)
- Harm reduction framing: Meet people where they are
- Bilingual materials
- Include rural communities (methamphetamine + fentanyl contamination)

**Channels**: Harm reduction organizations, syringe service programs, emergency departments, jails/prisons (at release), recovery meetings, pharmacies, social media, community events, libraries, laundromats

**Timeline**: Year-round with spikes around Overdose Awareness Day (Aug 31) and known high-risk periods

**Metrics**: # naloxone kits distributed, # overdose reversals reported, overdose death rate, # training events held

## Campaign 3: Maternal Health / Birth Equity

**Goal**: Reduce maternal mortality and morbidity with focus on Black maternal health

**Audiences**:
- Primary: Pregnant and postpartum people, especially Black women
- Secondary: OB/GYN providers, doulas, family members

**Key Messages**:
- "You deserve to be heard. If something doesn't feel right, speak up — and insist"
- "Free doula support is available to help you through pregnancy and birth"
- "Your health matters during pregnancy AND after — keep all postpartum appointments"

**Equity Lens**:
- Center Black women and birthing people in all messaging
- Name structural racism as a driver of maternal mortality
- Partner with Black doulas, midwives, and maternal health organizations
- Materials available in locations Black women trust (churches, salons, community centers)
- Address implicit bias in healthcare settings (provider-facing education)

**Channels**: Prenatal clinics, WIC offices, doula networks, churches, barbershops/salons, social media, community baby showers, hospital discharge packets

**Timeline**: Year-round with emphasis during Black Maternal Health Week (April) and Prematurity Awareness Month (November)

**Metrics**: Maternal mortality ratio (overall and by race), severe maternal morbidity rate, doula referral rate, postpartum visit completion rate

## Campaign 4: Mental Health / 988

**Goal**: Increase awareness and use of 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline

**Audiences**:
- Primary: People in crisis, their family/friends
- Secondary: Gatekeepers (teachers, coaches, faith leaders, employers)

**Key Messages**:
- "988 is here for you — call or text, 24/7, free and confidential"
- "Asking someone if they're thinking about suicide does NOT put the idea in their head — it could save their life"
- "You don't have to be suicidal to call — 988 is for any mental health crisis"

**Equity Lens**:
- Normalize mental health in communities where stigma is high
- Culturally specific messaging for Black, Latino, LGBTQ+, veteran, and rural communities
- Address barriers: distrust of systems, fear of involuntary hospitalization, cultural stigma
- Available in Spanish (press 2 for Spanish on 988)
- Veteran-specific messaging (press 1 for Veterans Crisis Line)

**Channels**: Schools, workplaces, faith communities, primary care offices, social media, transit ads, community events, crisis centers, libraries

**Timeline**: Year-round with emphasis during Suicide Prevention Month (September) and Mental Health Awareness Month (May)

**Metrics**: 988 call volume, ED visits for mental health crisis, suicide rate, gatekeeper training completion rate

## Campaign 5: Food Access / SNAP

**Goal**: Increase SNAP enrollment among eligible but unenrolled populations

**Audiences**:
- Primary: Eligible individuals/families not currently enrolled (seniors, working families, immigrants)
- Secondary: Social workers, community health workers, faith communities

**Key Messages**:
- "SNAP helps families put healthy food on the table — you may qualify"
- "SNAP does not affect your immigration status" (critical for immigrant communities)
- "Free help applying is available at [locations]"

**Equity Lens**:
- Address stigma around food assistance
- Bilingual application assistance
- Target senior populations (lowest enrollment rate among eligible groups)
- Clarify public charge rule — SNAP does NOT count
- Partner with food pantries, WIC, schools, senior centers

**Channels**: Food pantries, WIC offices, schools (free/reduced lunch families), senior centers, FQHC waiting rooms, social media, community events, faith communities

**Timeline**: Year-round with emphasis during summer (school meal gap) and holiday season

**Metrics**: SNAP enrollment rate, # applications assisted, # new enrollees, food insecurity rate (CHNA measure)

## Campaign 6: Lead Poisoning Prevention

**Goal**: Increase childhood blood lead level testing and reduce lead exposure

**Audiences**:
- Primary: Parents of children ages 0-6 (especially in pre-1978 housing)
- Secondary: Landlords, pediatricians, WIC nutritionists, home visitors

**Key Messages**:
- "No amount of lead is safe for children. A simple blood test can check"
- "If your home was built before 1978, it may have lead paint — free testing is available"
- "Your landlord is responsible for lead-safe housing"

**Equity Lens**:
- Lead exposure is concentrated in older housing in low-income, predominantly Black neighborhoods
- This is environmental racism — name it
- Tenant rights education (landlord responsibility for remediation)
- Testing accessible at WIC visits, community clinics, and home visits
- Free or low-cost remediation programs

**Channels**: Pediatric offices, WIC appointments, home visiting programs, tenant organizations, community health fairs, social media, school enrollment, housing authorities

**Timeline**: Year-round with emphasis during Lead Poisoning Prevention Week (October) and spring/summer (renovation/window-opening season)

**Metrics**: Blood lead level testing rate, # children with elevated BLL, # homes assessed, # homes remediated
