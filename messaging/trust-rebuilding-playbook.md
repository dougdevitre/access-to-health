# Trust-Rebuilding Playbook

> Strategies for rebuilding public trust in health institutions
> Covers: Local proxy model, HEAR framework, prebunking, misinformation response

---

## Why Trust Is Low

Trust in public health institutions has eroded due to:
- **Historical harm**: Tuskegee, forced sterilization, medical experimentation (see `populations/deep-dives/black-african-american.md`)
- **COVID-19 response**: Inconsistent messaging, perceived politicization, mandates without community input
- **Institutional failures**: Flint water crisis, opioid epidemic delayed response, environmental racism
- **Political polarization**: Health has become partisan — it shouldn't be, but it is
- **Information ecosystem**: Social media amplifies misinformation faster than corrections travel

**Key principle**: Distrust is often rational. It reflects real experience. The solution is not better messaging — it's better behavior, sustained over time, with accountability.

## The Local Proxy Model

### Concept
People trust people more than institutions. Instead of asking communities to trust the health department directly, work through **trusted local proxies** — people and organizations already embedded in community trust networks.

### Identifying Trusted Proxies

| Community | Trusted Proxies |
|-----------|----------------|
| Black communities | Black churches, barbers/stylists, Black physicians, Divine Nine organizations, community organizers |
| Hispanic/Latino | Promotoras, Catholic churches, Hispanic Chamber, Spanish-language media, tiendas/bodegas |
| Rural | Family doctors, farm bureaus, churches, local businesses, volunteer fire departments |
| Immigrant/refugee | Ethnic community organizations, resettlement agencies, cultural brokers, religious leaders |
| LGBTQ+ | LGBTQ+ community centers, Pride organizations, affirming healthcare providers |
| Youth | Coaches, teachers, older siblings/cousins, social media influencers (local), barbers |
| Older adults | Senior centers, Medicare counselors, faith communities, local pharmacists |

### How to Work With Proxies
1. **Build the relationship first** — before you need something. Show up, listen, learn
2. **Compensate fairly** — stipends, contracts, not just "volunteer" expectations
3. **Share power** — let them shape the message, not just deliver yours
4. **Provide resources** — training, materials, data, referral connections
5. **Follow through** — if they vouched for you, don't make them look bad
6. **Report back** — show them (and their community) what happened because of their involvement

## The HEAR Framework

### For Responding to Health Concerns and Hesitancy

**H — Hear the concern**
Listen fully without interrupting. Reflect it back. "I hear you saying that you're concerned about [X]."
Do NOT dismiss, correct, or counter immediately.

**E — Empathize**
Validate the emotion behind the concern. "That makes sense given [context/experience]."
Acknowledge historical reasons for distrust where relevant.

**A — Answer with evidence**
Share facts simply and clearly. Use trusted sources. Lead with what you DO know, acknowledge what you don't.
"Here's what the evidence shows..."
Keep it to 1-2 key facts. Don't data-dump.

**R — Recommend a next step**
Offer one specific, low-barrier action. "Would you be willing to [specific action]?"
Respect autonomy. The goal is informed decision-making, not compliance.

### HEAR in Practice

**Scenario**: Parent hesitant about childhood vaccines

| Step | What to Say |
|------|-------------|
| H | "Tell me what's on your mind about the vaccines." |
| E | "I understand. You want to make the best decision for your child, and there's a lot of information out there." |
| A | "The vaccines on the childhood schedule have been studied in millions of children. The risk of serious side effects is extremely low — much lower than the risk of the diseases they prevent." |
| R | "Would it help to spread the vaccines out over a few visits so we can go at your pace? Let's schedule the most important ones first." |

## Prebunking vs. Debunking

### Prebunking (Proactive — Preferred)
Inoculate people against misinformation BEFORE they encounter it.

**Technique: The Truth Sandwich**
1. State the fact first: "Vaccines are safe and effective for children"
2. Warn about the myth: "You may see claims online that vaccines cause [X]"
3. Explain the technique: "These claims use cherry-picked data from non-medical sources"
4. Restate the fact: "Large-scale studies consistently show vaccines are safe"

**Key principle**: Lead with truth. Never lead with the myth (it reinforces it).

### Debunking (Reactive — When Misinformation Is Already Circulating)

**Technique: The Fact-Myth-Fallacy-Fact Framework**
1. **Fact**: State the correct information
2. **Myth**: Warn that a myth exists (briefly — don't amplify)
3. **Fallacy**: Explain WHY the myth is wrong (the logical or evidential flaw)
4. **Fact**: Restate the correct information with a simple visual or data point

### Social Media Misinformation Response Protocol
1. **Assess**: Is this misinformation gaining traction? (shares, comments, local reach)
2. **Don't amplify**: Do NOT share, quote-tweet, or screenshot the misinformation
3. **Respond with facts**: Post factual content on the same topic (not a direct reply unless the post has significant reach)
4. **Use trusted voices**: Ask proxies to share accurate information from their accounts
5. **Report**: Report to platform if it violates health misinformation policies
6. **Document**: Screenshot and log for after-action review

## Crisis Trust Recovery

### When Trust Has Been Broken (After an Incident)

**Phase 1: Acknowledge (Immediately)**
- Name what happened. Do not minimize.
- "We made a mistake" or "We should have done this differently"
- Accept responsibility — don't blame external factors

**Phase 2: Apologize (Within 24-48 Hours)**
- Genuine apology: "We're sorry. This should not have happened."
- Specific: What exactly are you sorry for?
- No "I'm sorry you feel that way" — that's not an apology

**Phase 3: Act (Within 1-2 Weeks)**
- Concrete changes: What are you doing differently?
- Visible accountability: Who is responsible for the fix?
- Community input: Ask those affected what they need

**Phase 4: Report Back (Ongoing)**
- Provide updates on changes made
- Invite ongoing feedback
- Demonstrate sustained change — not just a one-time fix

## Community Trust Assessment

### Quick Assessment Questions
Ask community members and partners:
1. Do you trust the health department to tell the truth about health risks? (1-5)
2. Do you trust the health department to treat all communities fairly? (1-5)
3. Do you feel the health department listens to your community? (1-5)
4. Would you follow a health recommendation from the health department? (1-5)
5. Do you feel welcome at health department programs and events? (1-5)

**Scoring**: Average < 3 = significant trust deficit. Investigate root causes before launching campaigns.

### Trust-Building Actions Checklist
- [ ] Community advisory board with real decision-making power
- [ ] Staff reflect the demographic composition of the community
- [ ] Programs designed WITH communities, not FOR them
- [ ] Data shared publicly and in plain language
- [ ] Accountability reports published (what we promised vs. what we delivered)
- [ ] Community members compensated for their time and expertise
- [ ] Historical harms acknowledged, not avoided
- [ ] Consistent presence in the community (not just during crises or grant cycles)
