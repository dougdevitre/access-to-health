> Every outward-facing communication artifact: media, public, digital, partner, regulatory
> Organized by channel and audience

## Section 1: Media Communication

### 1.1 Media Inquiry Response Protocol
```
STEP 1: Log the inquiry
Reporter name: | Outlet: | Phone/email: | Deadline: | Topic:

STEP 2: Assess urgency
□ Breaking/crisis (respond within 1 hour)
□ Deadline same day (respond within 2 hours)
□ Feature/investigative (respond within 24 hours)
□ Routine (respond within 48 hours)

STEP 3: Route
□ HCS drafts response
□ Subject matter expert provides content (EPI for data, HDO for policy, PHN for clinical)
□ HDO approves all statements during crisis
□ Legal review if litigation-adjacent

STEP 4: Respond
□ On the record (attributed to named spokesperson)
□ On background (attributed to "health department official")
□ Off the record (not for publication — use sparingly and only with trusted reporters)

STEP 5: Document
File inquiry, response, and any follow-up in media log.
```

### 1.2 Media Interview Preparation Sheet
```
INTERVIEW PREP
Spokesperson: [Name, Title]
Reporter: [Name, Outlet]
Date/Time: [Date] | Format: [Phone / In-person / Live TV / Recorded]
Topic: [Topic]
Estimated length: [# minutes]

KEY MESSAGES (Memorize these — always return to them)
1. [Message 1 — most important]
2. [Message 2]
3. [Message 3]

SUPPORTING DATA
- [Stat 1 with source]
- [Stat 2 with source]
- [Local data point]

ANTICIPATED QUESTIONS
Q: [Expected question 1]
A: [Prepared answer — bridge back to key message]

Q: [Expected question 2]
A: [Prepared answer]

Q: [Difficult/gotcha question]
A: [Bridge: "That's an important question, and what I can tell you is..." → key message]

DO:
✅ Speak in complete sentences (sound bites that can stand alone)
✅ Use plain language (no jargon, no acronyms)
✅ Repeat key messages (reporters use what they hear most)
✅ Say "I don't know, but I'll find out" rather than guess
✅ Reference local impact and real people (de-identified)

DON'T:
❌ Say "no comment" (say "I can't speak to that, but what I can tell you is...")
❌ Speculate beyond what data supports
❌ Go off the record unless you trust the reporter AND have a strategic reason
❌ Argue or get defensive
❌ Use medical jargon
❌ Say anything you wouldn't want on the front page
```

## Section 2: Digital and Social Media

### 2.1 Website Content Standards
```
WEBSITE CONTENT STANDARDS

PAGE STRUCTURE:
- Title (H1): Clear, searchable, plain language
- Summary (first paragraph): Answer who, what, when, where, why in 2-3 sentences
- Body: Short paragraphs (3-4 sentences max). Subheadings every 2-3 paragraphs
- Call to action: Every page ends with a clear next step
- Contact: Name, phone, email on every page
- Last updated: Date visible on every page

READABILITY:
- 6th grade reading level for public pages (check with Hemingway Editor or Flesch-Kincaid)
- Define technical terms on first use
- Active voice ("We provide" not "Services are provided")
- Short sentences (15-20 words average)
- Bulleted lists for 3+ items

ACCESSIBILITY (WCAG 2.1 AA):
- Alt text on all images (descriptive, not decorative)
- Color contrast ratio ≥4.5:1 for text
- Keyboard navigable
- Screen reader compatible
- Captions on all video
- Downloadable documents in accessible PDF format

MULTILINGUAL:
- Top 3 languages in the service area available for vital content
- "Select language" prominently visible
- Don't rely on Google Translate for health content — professional translation required

SEO:
- Title tags: [Topic] — [Health Department Name]
- Meta descriptions: 150-160 characters, include key action and location
- Header hierarchy: H1 → H2 → H3 (never skip levels)
- Internal linking between related pages
```

### 2.2 Social Media Crisis Protocol
```
SOCIAL MEDIA CRISIS COMMUNICATION PROTOCOL

MONITORING (Always On):
- Check mentions and comments 3x daily minimum (9 AM, 12 PM, 4 PM)
- Set alerts for: [Health department name], [health director name], outbreak terms, community name + health
- Monitor for: Misinformation, rumors, viral complaints, emerging crises

WHEN CRISIS HITS:

FIRST 30 MINUTES:
□ Pause all scheduled posts (prevent tone-deaf content from auto-posting)
□ Notify HCS lead and HDO
□ Draft holding statement for social media
□ Post acknowledgment: "We're aware of [situation]. We're gathering information and will update you shortly."

FIRST 2 HOURS:
□ Post substantive update with: What we know, what we're doing, what you should do, when we'll update next
□ Pin post to top of Facebook page
□ Monitor comments — respond to direct questions factually. Do NOT argue
□ Flag and report: Threats, doxxing, dangerous misinformation
□ Coordinate messaging with partner agencies (shared language)

ONGOING:
□ Update posts at regular intervals (every 2-4 hours during acute phase)
□ Respond to misinformation with factual correction (not defensive) within 1 hour
□ Share resources and guidance repeatedly (people miss first post)
□ Use consistent hashtag for the event
□ Document all posts and comments (screenshot) for after-action review

POST-CRISIS:
□ Summary post: What happened, what we did, what's next
□ Thank community and partners
□ Direct to ongoing resources
□ Resume normal posting schedule
□ After-action review: What worked? What broke? What do we change?

RULES FOR CRISIS SOCIAL MEDIA:
✅ Be first (beat the rumor mill)
✅ Be factual (never speculate)
✅ Be empathetic ("We understand your concern")
✅ Be human (not robotic institutional voice)
✅ Admit what you don't know ("We're still investigating")
❌ Never delete comments (unless threats/harassment/HIPAA violation)
❌ Never argue with commenters
❌ Never go dark (silence breeds conspiracy)
❌ Never blame the community
```

### 2.3 Email Communication Standards
```
PUBLIC EMAIL COMMUNICATION STANDARDS

SUBJECT LINES:
- Action-oriented: "Free flu shots this Saturday" not "Immunization update"
- Under 50 characters when possible
- No ALL CAPS (spam filter trigger)
- Include location or date for events

FROM NAME:
"[Health Department Name]" not "noreply@" or an individual name

STRUCTURE:
1. Purpose statement (first sentence — why this email matters)
2. Key information (what you need to know)
3. Action item (what to do — ONE clear CTA)
4. Resources (links, phone numbers)
5. Unsubscribe link (CAN-SPAM compliance)

FREQUENCY:
- Community newsletter: Monthly (max biweekly)
- Event promotions: 2 weeks before, 1 week before, day before
- Health advisories: As needed (not more than weekly unless crisis)
- Re-engagement: 90 days of inactivity → check-in. 120 days → final confirmation

SEGMENTATION:
Segment lists by: Geographic area, language preference, topic interest, role (provider vs. community member), engagement level. Don't send everything to everyone.

METRICS TO TRACK:
- Open rate: Target 25-35% (nonprofit/health benchmark)
- Click rate: Target 3-5%
- Unsubscribe rate: Ceiling 0.5% per send (>1% = review content and frequency)
- Bounce rate: Clean list quarterly, remove hard bounces immediately
```

## Section 4: Community Presentation Toolkit

### 4.1 Community Presentation Template (for Non-Expert Audiences)
```
PRESENTING HEALTH DATA TO COMMUNITY MEMBERS
A Facilitation Guide

BEFORE THE PRESENTATION:
□ Know your audience: Age range, education levels, languages, cultural context
□ Pre-test your materials with someone from the community
□ Arrange: Interpretation, childcare, transportation, food, accessible venue
□ Prepare handouts: Plain language, large font, bilingual, with contact info

SLIDE DESIGN RULES FOR COMMUNITY:
- Maximum 15 slides for a 45-minute presentation
- Large font (minimum 24pt)
- One chart per slide with interpretation below
- Photos of real community (with consent) — not stock photos
- No acronyms without explanation
- Color palette: High contrast, colorblind-friendly

PRESENTATION STRUCTURE:
1. Welcome and purpose (5 min) — "We're here because your voice matters"
2. Who are we? (3 min) — Brief, human, not institutional
3. What the data shows (15 min) — 3-5 key findings, plain language, visual
4. What we're doing about it (10 min) — Specific programs, not promises
5. What do you think? (15 min) — Open discussion, collect input
6. Next steps and how to stay involved (5 min)
7. Resources and contact (2 min)

FACILITATION TIPS:
- Start by acknowledging the community's expertise on their own health
- Use "we" not "you" — "In our community..." not "Your community has a problem with..."
- Present challenges AND strengths/assets
- When showing disparity data: Name structural causes. Never imply individual or cultural blame
- Leave time for discussion — don't lecture for 55 minutes and ask "any questions?" at the end
- End with hope and action — not despair

POST-PRESENTATION:
□ Thank attendees
□ Distribute resource packets and contact information
□ Collect feedback (paper evaluation + online option)
□ Follow up within 2 weeks with summary of what was heard and next steps
```

### 4.2 Data Storytelling Framework
```
DATA STORYTELLING: TURNING NUMBERS INTO ACTION

THE 3-ACT STRUCTURE FOR HEALTH DATA:

ACT 1: THE HOOK (What's happening?)
"In our community, [X people/families] are affected by [issue]."
Show ONE compelling number. Make it local and personal.
Example: "Last year, 47 babies in our county were born too early. That's nearly one a week."

ACT 2: THE CONTEXT (Why is it happening?)
"This is connected to [structural factor], which means [specific mechanism]."
Show the trend (better/worse/stable). Show the comparison (us vs. state/nation). Show the disparity.
Example: "Preterm birth is twice as common in zip codes with the highest poverty rates. This isn't because of individual choices — it's because of decades of underinvestment in these neighborhoods."

ACT 3: THE PATH FORWARD (What can we do?)
"Here's what works, and here's what we're doing."
Show evidence-based intervention. Show local program. Show results.
Example: "Community doula programs reduce preterm birth by 30%. Our new doula program connected 89 families with support last year."

CLOSE: THE ASK
"Here's one thing you can do."
Specific, actionable, time-bound.
Example: "If you know a pregnant person who needs support, share this number: [phone]."

RULES:
1. Lead with people, not percentages
2. One story per chart. If you need a paragraph to explain a chart, simplify the chart
3. Say "people" not "cases" or "subjects"
4. Always give the audience an action — never leave them with just a problem
5. Test your story with a non-expert before presenting
```
