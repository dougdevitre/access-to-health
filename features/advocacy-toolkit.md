# Advocacy Toolkit

> Scripts, testimony templates, coalition tools, and media advocacy for public health policy change
> For use by: POL, HDO, CES, HCS, and any role engaging in policy advocacy

## Testimony Template

### Written Testimony for Legislative Committee

```
TESTIMONY OF [Name, Title, Credentials]
[Organization]
Before the [Committee Name]
[State House / Senate]
Regarding: [Bill Number] — [Bill Title]
[Date]

Chairman [Name], Vice Chair [Name], and members of the committee:

My name is [Name], and I am the [Title] at [Organization]. I am here today to
[SUPPORT / OPPOSE / REQUEST AMENDMENTS TO] [Bill Number].

WHAT THIS BILL DOES
[1-2 sentences — factual summary of the bill's impact on public health]

WHY IT MATTERS
[2-3 sentences — local data, human impact, health equity implications]
- In [jurisdiction], [local data point with source]
- [Population] is disproportionately affected because [structural reason]
- [Evidence of what works from other states/research]

WHAT WE RECOMMEND
[Specific ask — pass as written, amend section X, appropriate $X, etc.]

CLOSING
[1-2 sentences — restate position and offer to be a resource]

I am happy to answer questions and can provide additional data upon request.

[Name] | [Title] | [Phone] | [Email]
```

### Oral Testimony Tips (2-3 minutes max)
1. State name, title, organization, and position in the first 15 seconds
2. Lead with ONE local data point that surprises
3. Tell ONE brief story (de-identified, with consent) that makes the data human
4. Make ONE specific ask
5. Thank the committee
6. Do NOT read your written testimony — speak from notes
7. Dress professionally. Bring copies for the committee

## Legislator Visit Guide

### Before the Visit
- Research the legislator's committees, voting record, and public statements on health
- Identify common ground (their district's health data, their constituents' needs)
- Prepare a 1-page leave-behind with: local data, your ask, and contact information
- Assign roles: Lead speaker, data person, story-teller, note-taker
- Practice the ask out loud

### During the Visit (20-30 minutes typical)
1. **Thank them** for their time and any past support for health (2 min)
2. **Frame the issue** with local data from their district (3 min)
3. **Tell the story** — one constituent story (de-identified, consensual) (3 min)
4. **Make the ask** — specific, actionable, time-bound (2 min)
5. **Listen** — what are their concerns? What would help them support this? (10 min)
6. **Close** — summarize the ask, offer follow-up data, thank them (2 min)

### After the Visit
- Send thank-you email within 24 hours
- Provide any data they requested within 1 week
- Track the legislator's actions on the issue
- Report back to your coalition
- Follow up before key votes

## Media Advocacy

### Op-Ed Strategy
Use op-eds to: build public support, respond to opposition, frame an issue before a vote, or celebrate a win.

**Placement priority**:
1. Largest circulation paper in your jurisdiction
2. Paper read by the target decision-maker
3. Online-only outlets with relevant audience
4. Trade/professional publications (for workforce issues)

See `communication/external-playbook.md` Section 1.3 for full op-ed submission template.

### Letter to the Editor (Rapid Response)
- 150-200 words max
- Reference the article/editorial you're responding to (by date and title)
- State your point in the first sentence
- Include ONE data point
- Close with a call to action
- Submit within 24-48 hours of the original piece (timeliness is critical)

### Social Media Advocacy
- **Do**: Share data visualizations, link to evidence, amplify community voices, use relevant hashtags
- **Don't**: Attack individuals, use partisan language, share confidential information, make promises you can't keep
- **Platform strategy**: Twitter/X for legislators and media; Facebook for community; Instagram for younger audiences; LinkedIn for professional/workforce issues

## Grassroots Mobilization

### Action Alert Template

```
URGENT: [Action Needed] by [Date]

[1-2 sentences: What's happening and why it matters]

WHAT YOU CAN DO:
1. Call [Legislator] at [phone]: "I'm a constituent and I [support/oppose]
   [bill] because [health reason]."
2. Email: [pre-drafted email or link]
3. Share this alert with your network

KEY TALKING POINTS:
- [Point 1 — local impact]
- [Point 2 — who is affected]
- [Point 3 — what the evidence says]

DEADLINE: [Vote date / Comment period end / Hearing date]

Questions? Contact [Name] at [email/phone]
```

### Community Town Hall Planning
1. Partner with community organization (not just health department-hosted)
2. Accessible venue (ADA, transit, parking, childcare, interpretation)
3. Invite the legislator or decision-maker to attend and listen
4. Structure: 20% presentation, 80% community voice
5. Document everything — the legislator needs to hear directly from constituents
6. Follow up with a summary to the decision-maker within 1 week

## Health Impact Assessment (HIA) — Quick Reference

### When to Conduct an HIA
- A non-health policy, project, or program is being proposed that may affect health
- Examples: Highway expansion, zoning change, school closure, minimum wage bill, housing development

### 5 Steps
1. **Screening**: Will this policy affect health? Is an HIA feasible and timely?
2. **Scoping**: Which health effects? Which populations? What data do we need?
3. **Assessment**: Analyze baseline health, predict impacts, assess distribution (equity)
4. **Recommendations**: Propose changes to maximize health benefits and minimize harm
5. **Monitoring**: Track whether recommendations were adopted and health outcomes changed

### HIA Report Structure
- Executive summary (1 page)
- Background and policy description
- Health determinant pathway diagram
- Baseline health data
- Impact predictions by population group
- Equity analysis
- Recommendations (prioritized, specific, feasible)
- Monitoring plan
