# City Planning and Health — Built Environment, Zoning, and Transportation

> For city planners, urban designers, transportation planners, housing authorities, and parks departments
> How land use decisions become health outcomes — and how to plan for health equity

---

## Why City Planners Need This

Every planning decision is a health decision:
- **Zoning** determines where people live, work, and access food and healthcare
- **Transportation** determines who can reach jobs, schools, clinics, and grocery stores
- **Housing** determines exposure to lead, mold, overcrowding, and displacement
- **Parks and green space** determine physical activity, mental health, and air quality
- **Land use** determines proximity to pollution, noise, and environmental hazards

**The connection**: Zip code is a better predictor of health than genetic code. Planners shape zip codes.

## Health Impact Assessment (HIA) for Planning Decisions

### When to Conduct an HIA

Trigger an HIA when a planning decision may affect health:
- Comprehensive plan update
- Zoning code amendment
- Transportation project (highway, transit, bike/ped)
- Housing development or redevelopment
- Park or recreation facility
- School siting or closure
- Industrial or commercial development
- Environmental remediation

### HIA 5-Step Process

| Step | Activities | Health Department Role |
|------|-----------|----------------------|
| **1. Screening** | Will this decision affect health? Is an HIA feasible and timely? | Provide health expertise to determine scope |
| **2. Scoping** | Which health effects? Which populations? What data? | Identify health determinant pathways |
| **3. Assessment** | Analyze baseline health, predict impacts, assess equity | Provide health data, epidemiologic analysis |
| **4. Recommendations** | Propose changes to maximize health benefits | Draft health-protective recommendations |
| **5. Monitoring** | Track whether recommendations were adopted and outcomes changed | Monitor health indicators post-implementation |

### HIA Health Determinant Pathways

```
Planning Decision
    │
    ├── Air quality ──────── Respiratory disease, cardiovascular, cancer
    ├── Noise ────────────── Sleep disruption, stress, cardiovascular
    ├── Physical activity ── Obesity, diabetes, mental health, falls
    ├── Food access ──────── Nutrition, chronic disease, food insecurity
    ├── Social cohesion ──── Mental health, violence, civic engagement
    ├── Safety ───────────── Pedestrian injury, crime, domestic violence
    ├── Housing quality ──── Lead, asthma triggers, thermal stress
    ├── Displacement ─────── Stress, homelessness, loss of social networks
    ├── Economic opportunity  Income, insurance, education access
    └── Healthcare access ── Preventive care, chronic disease management
```

See `features/advocacy-toolkit.md` for the full HIA reference and report structure.

## Health-Informed Zoning

### Zoning and Health Connections

| Zoning Element | Health Impact | Health-Protective Approach |
|---------------|--------------|---------------------------|
| **Land use mix** | Single-use zoning → car dependence → obesity, air pollution, isolation | Mixed-use development: housing near jobs, transit, services, food |
| **Density** | Low density → sprawl → driving → pollution, pedestrian danger, isolation | Moderate density near transit. Walkable neighborhoods reduce obesity by 10-15% |
| **Setbacks and buffers** | Housing near highways → asthma, cancer, low birth weight | 500+ foot buffer between highways and residences, schools, childcare |
| **Food environment** | Zoning that allows fast food but not grocery → food desert | Healthy food zoning: incentivize grocery, restrict fast food density near schools |
| **Alcohol outlet density** | More outlets per capita → more violence, DUI, chronic liver disease | Cap alcohol outlet density. Require distance from schools and churches |
| **Green space** | No park access → less activity, worse mental health, more heat | Require parkland dedication. 10-minute walk to park for all residents |
| **Industrial proximity** | Residential near industrial → pollution exposure, cancer, respiratory | Industrial buffer zones. Environmental justice review for permits |
| **Parking minimums** | More parking → more driving → more pollution, less walkability | Reduce/eliminate parking minimums near transit. Prioritize bike/ped infrastructure |

### Health Equity Overlay Zone (Model)

Create a **Health Equity Overlay Zone** for areas with the worst health outcomes:

```
HEALTH EQUITY OVERLAY ZONE

Applies to: Census tracts in the lowest quartile of life expectancy OR
            highest quartile of health disparity index

Additional requirements within overlay:
1. New residential development must include ≥15% affordable units
2. Fast food restaurants require conditional use permit (not by-right)
3. Full-service grocery store incentivized (tax abatement, reduced fees)
4. New industrial uses require HIA before approval
5. Alcohol outlet density capped at [#] per [area]
6. 10-minute walk to park or green space required for all new residential
7. Complete streets standards apply to all road projects
8. Environmental contamination assessment required for all development
9. Community advisory review for projects >$[threshold]
10. Anti-displacement measures required for publicly subsidized projects
```

## Transportation and Health

### How Transportation Decisions Affect Health

| Transportation Factor | Health Pathway | Who Is Most Affected |
|----------------------|---------------|---------------------|
| **Car dependence** | Physical inactivity → obesity, diabetes, CVD | Low-income, elderly, disabled, youth |
| **Air pollution** | Vehicle emissions → asthma, COPD, cancer, low birth weight | Communities near highways, transit corridors |
| **Pedestrian safety** | Dangerous roads → pedestrian/cyclist fatalities | Children, elderly, communities of color, low-income |
| **Transit access** | No transit → can't reach jobs, healthcare, food | Transit-dependent populations (low-income, disabled, elderly) |
| **Active transportation** | Walking/biking infrastructure → physical activity → better health | All populations; equity depends on infrastructure investment |
| **Commute stress** | Long commutes → poor mental health, less family time, less sleep | Suburban and exurban workers, low-wage workers |

### Health-Protective Transportation Recommendations

1. **Complete Streets**: Design every road for all users (pedestrians, cyclists, transit, cars, disability)
2. **Vision Zero**: Eliminate traffic fatalities through design, not just enforcement
3. **Transit equity**: Route and frequency decisions should prioritize communities with lowest car ownership and worst health outcomes
4. **Active transportation**: Protected bike lanes, sidewalk completion, trail networks
5. **Anti-displacement**: Transit investment must include anti-displacement measures (affordable housing, community benefits agreements)
6. **Air quality**: Buffer housing from highways. Electrify transit. Plant urban tree canopy
7. **Health data in planning**: Use health data (ED visits, chronic disease rates, pedestrian injuries) in transportation planning models

### Transportation Access and Health Outcomes (Data Points)
- People with reliable transportation are 2x more likely to keep medical appointments
- Communities with walkable infrastructure have 10-15% lower obesity rates
- Proximity to a transit stop is associated with higher preventive care utilization
- Pedestrian fatality rates are 2x higher in communities of color
- A 10% increase in active commuting is associated with a 14% reduction in CVD risk

## Housing and Health

### Housing as Health Infrastructure

| Housing Condition | Health Impact | Planning/Policy Response |
|------------------|--------------|--------------------------|
| **Lead paint** (pre-1978) | Brain damage, developmental delays in children | Require lead inspection at sale/rental. Fund remediation. See `messaging/campaign-builder.md` Campaign 6 |
| **Mold/moisture** | Asthma, respiratory illness | Require minimum maintenance standards. Fund weatherization |
| **Overcrowding** | Infectious disease, mental health, domestic violence | Affordable housing production. Anti-displacement |
| **Thermal stress** | Heat-related illness/death, hypothermia | Weatherization. Cooling/warming centers. Green infrastructure |
| **Instability/eviction** | Depression, substance use, poor birth outcomes, homelessness | Eviction prevention. Right to counsel. Emergency rental assistance |
| **Homelessness** | 12+ year life expectancy reduction. All health conditions worsen | Housing First. Permanent supportive housing. Coordinated Entry |
| **Displacement/gentrification** | Loss of social networks, stress, reduced healthcare access | Inclusionary zoning. Community benefits agreements. Community land trusts |

### Healthy Housing Code (Model Standards)
- Smoke-free multi-unit housing
- Lead-safe certification for pre-1978 rental units
- Mold/moisture remediation standards
- Minimum temperature requirements (heating and cooling)
- Pest management (IPM) standards
- CO detector and smoke detector requirements
- Radon testing for ground-contact units
- Accessibility standards beyond ADA minimums

## Parks, Green Space, and Health

### Evidence
- Access to green space reduces depression by 25-30%
- Urban tree canopy reduces summer temperatures by 2-9��F
- 150 minutes/week of physical activity reduces all-cause mortality by 33%
- Park proximity is associated with reduced childhood obesity
- Green space reduces cortisol (stress hormone) levels

### Equity Standard
- **10-minute walk to a park** for every resident (Trust for Public Land standard)
- Prioritize investment in communities with least access and worst health outcomes
- Design for all ages and abilities (universal design)
- Community input on park design and programming (nothing about us without us)
- Address safety concerns (lighting, sight lines, maintenance, programming)
- Parks as health infrastructure: host screenings, vaccination clinics, health education, farmers markets

## Cross-Sector Data for Planners

### Health Data Sources Useful for Planning

| Data Source | What It Tells You | Geography |
|-------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **CDC PLACES** | Chronic disease, health behaviors, prevention at census tract level | Census tract |
| **County Health Rankings** | Health outcomes and health factors by county | County |
| **EPA EJScreen** | Environmental justice indicators (pollution, demographics) | Census tract/block group |
| **Census ACS** | Demographics, income, housing, transportation, insurance | Block group and up |
| **BRFSS** | Behavioral risk factors (state, some metro) | State/metro |
| **Vital statistics** | Births, deaths, cause of death by geography | County, ZIP |
| **Hospital discharge data** | ED visits, hospitalizations by condition and geography | ZIP, county |
| **Walk Score / Transit Score** | Walkability and transit access | Address level |
| **USDA Food Access Research Atlas** | Food desert identification | Census tract |

### Requesting Health Data from the Health Department

Health departments can provide:
- Disease rates by geography (de-identified)
- SDOH screening aggregates by ZIP/census tract
- Community Health Needs Assessment data and findings
- Lead testing data by geography
- Food inspection results
- Environmental health complaint data

**Process**: Submit a data request (see `scripts/team-sops.md` SOP 6). Allow 2-4 weeks. De-identified data is generally available. Individual-level data requires data sharing agreements and IRB review.

## Collaboration Framework

### How Planners and Health Departments Can Work Together

| Opportunity | Planning Action | Health Department Role |
|-------------|----------------|----------------------|
| **Comprehensive plan** | Include health chapter/element | Provide data, draft health goals, review plan |
| **Zoning update** | Health-informed zoning (food, alcohol, buffers) | HIA for proposed changes, health data |
| **Capital improvement plan** | Prioritize health-impacting infrastructure | Provide health equity data for prioritization |
| **Transportation plan** | Complete Streets, transit equity, active transportation | Pedestrian injury data, health access analysis |
| **Housing plan** | Healthy housing standards, anti-displacement | Lead data, housing-health connection data |
| **Parks plan** | 10-minute walk standard, health programming | Community health data, screening at parks |
| **Climate action plan** | Heat vulnerability, air quality, resilience | Health vulnerability assessment, heat illness data |
| **Hazard mitigation plan** | Health infrastructure protection | Public health emergency planning integration |

### Joint Products
- **Community Health Needs Assessment + Community Needs Assessment**: Merge or align CHNA (health department) with comprehensive plan community input
- **Health Equity Index**: Joint mapping of health, economic, and environmental indicators
- **Health in All Policies (HiAP) framework**: Institutionalize health review in all planning decisions
